Exercise 51.2

a) As \( I \) is a convex subset of \( \Re\), a straight line homotopy always exists between any two functions \( f,g : X \rightarrow I\). Hence \( [X,I]\) has a single element.

b) Y is path-connected,
\( \Rightarrow \forall \; y_0, y_1 \in Y \quad \exists \; \text{path} \; \gamma \quad \text{such that} \; \gamma(0) = y_0, \gamma(1) = y_1. \) We can use these paths to construct a homotopy between \( f(i) \; and \; g(i) \) for example, \begin{align} H : &I \times I \rightarrow Y, \\ H : &(i,t) \mapsto \gamma_i(t), \end{align} where, \( \gamma_i(t) \; \text{is a path such that} \; \gamma_i(0) = f(0) \; and \; \gamma_i(t) = g(t) \).
This homotopy is continuous in t but it does not ensure continuity in i.
Thus, we use the fact that \( [I,I] \) has a single element, which implies that the identity map \( i : I \rightarrow I\) and a constant map \( c_{x_0} : I \rightarrow 0 \) are homotopic to each other. Suppose \( f : I \rightarrow Y\), then \( f \; o \; i (: I \rightarrow Y) \simeq f \; o \;\; c_{x_0} (: I \rightarrow f(x_0) ) \). Therefore, any map \( g : I \rightarrow Y\) is null homotopic (to \(g(x_0)\)). Now, as Y is path connected, we can find a homotopy (using the path) between \( f(x_0) \; \text{and} \;g(x_0)\). To summarise, we have that for any \( f,g : I \rightarrow Y\), \begin{align} f(x) &\simeq f(x_0), \\ f(x_0) &\simeq g(x_0), \\ g(x_0) &\simeq g(x) \\ \Rightarrow f(x) &\simeq g(x). \end{align} Therefore \( [I,Y ]\) has a single element for any Y.

Exercise 52.3

Suppose \(f,g\) are two loops at \( x_0\), and \(\pi_1(X,x_0)\)is abelian. We need to prove that \( \hat{\alpha} = \hat{\beta}\), where \( \alpha,\; \beta \) are paths from \( x_0 \) to \( x_1\). We have that

\begin{align} f*g =& g*f \\ \Rightarrow \bar{\alpha}* f * g *\alpha =& \bar{\alpha} * g * f * \alpha. \end{align}

Similarly,

\( \bar{\beta}* f * g * \beta = \bar{\beta} * g * f * \beta\).

Any loop can be decomposed into two paths, therefore we can suppose that \( f = \alpha* \bar{\beta}\), which is a valid loop at \( x_0\). Then upon substituting f in the above equation and using \( \alpha * \bar{\alpha} = e_{x_0}\), we get - \begin{align} \bar{\beta} * g * \alpha &= \bar{\alpha} * g * \alpha * \bar{\beta}* \alpha \\ &\text{and} \\ \bar{\beta} * \alpha * \bar{\beta} * g * \beta &= \bar{\beta} * g * \alpha. \end{align}

This gives us \begin{equation} \bar{\alpha} * (g * \alpha * \bar{\beta}) * \alpha = \bar{\beta} *( \alpha * \bar{\beta} * g )* \beta \end{equation}

As \( \pi_1(X,x_0)\) is abelian, \( g*(\alpha*\bar{\beta}) = ( \alpha* \bar{\beta})* g\) \( \Rightarrow \hat{\alpha} = \hat{\beta}\).

For the other implication, we suppose that \( \hat{\alpha} = \hat{\beta}\), and prove that \( \pi_1(X,x_0) \) is abelian. \\ We follow similar steps, let \(f,g\) be two loops at \(x_0\). Write down expanded version of following equations - \begin{align} \hat{\alpha} o (f * g) = \hat{\beta} o(f*g) \\ \hat{\alpha} o (g*f) = \hat{\beta} o (g*f) \\ \end{align} Again, suppose that \( f = \alpha * \bar{\beta}\), and substitute that in the above equations. This provides us with \begin{equation} \hat{\alpha} o (g * f) = \hat{\beta} o (f * g). \end{equation} Which implies that \( \pi_1(X,x_0)\) is abelian.

To prove - \begin{equation} r_* : \pi_1(X,a_0) \to \pi_1(A,a_0) \end{equation} is surjective.

Proof -

\( r : X \to A\) is a retraction. If \( \lambda\) is a loop in X, then \( r \; o\; \lambda \) is a loop in A. Suppose, \( \gamma\) is a loop in A at \( a_0\). Now, as \( A \subset X\), \( \gamma\) should be a loop in X as well, therefore \(r_* \) will be an identity in that case \( id : \gamma (\in \pi_1(X,a_0)) \to \gamma (\in \pi_1(A,a_0))\). Thus, every loop at \( a_0\) in A has a corresponding loop at \(a_0\) in X \( \Rightarrow r_* : \pi_1(X,a_0) \to \pi_1(A,a_0)) \) is surjective.

a)

\( (f \otimes g) (s) = f(s) . g(s)\), \( f,g \in \Omega(G,x_0)\) ( set of loops at \( x_0\)). \( (f \otimes g) (0) = f(0) . g(0) = x_0 . x_0 = x_0 = (f \otimes g) (1) \). This shows that \( (f \otimes g) (s) \) is again a loop at \( X_0\). Therefore, \( \Omega(G,x_0)\) is a group under \( \otimes\).

b)

Let \( [\gamma] \ne [\lambda] \in \pi_1 (G,x_0)\). Suppose, \( \exists \gamma_1, \gamma_2 \in [\gamma], and \lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in [\lambda]\). We need to show that \( [\gamma_1] \otimes [\lambda_1] = [\gamma_2] \otimes [\lambda_2] \); i.e.,

\begin{equation} \gamma_1 (s) . \lambda_1(s) \simeq \gamma_2(s) . \lambda_2(s). \end{equation}

The homotopy between these products can be found by taking production of the corresponding homotopies. Let, \( H : I \times I \to G, \text{ such that } H (s,0) = \gamma_1(s), \text{ and } H (s,1) = \gamma_2(s)\) be a continuous function \( H(s,t) = \gamma_t(s)\). Similarly, let \(F\) be the homotopy between \( \lambda_1, \lambda_2 \), \( F(t,s) = \lambda_t(s)\).

Then the homotopy needed is given by \( H(s,t) . F(s,t) = \gamma_t(s) . \lambda_t(s)\). Thus, \( \gamma_1 (s) \cdot \lambda_1(s) \simeq \gamma_2(s) \cdot \lambda_2(s). \)

c)

To show that \( \otimes \) is the same as \( *\), we first calculate

\begin{align*} (f * e_{x_0}) \otimes ( e_{x_0} * g) = &f * e_{x_0} (s) \cdot e_{x_0} * g (s) \\ =&\begin{cases} f(2s) \cdot e_{x_0}(2s) & \text{if } s \in [0, 1/2] \\ e_{x_0}(2s-1) \cdot g(2s-1) & \text{if } s \in [1/2, 1] \end{cases}\\ =& f * g \end{align*}

We know that \( f* e_{x_0} \simeq f\) (property of \( *\)). Thus we get that in \( \pi_1(G,x_0), f \otimes g = f * g\).

d)

We need to prove that \( \pi_1(G,x_0)\) is abelian, i.e., \( \forall [\gamma_1], [\gamma_2] \in \pi_1(G,x_0), \text{we have } [\gamma_1] \otimes [\gamma_2] = [\gamma_2] \otimes [\gamma_1]\).

Let, \( \gamma_1, \gamma_2\) be the representative of the respective equivalence classes. As discussed in the previous part, \( [\gamma_1] \otimes [\gamma_2] = \gamma_1 * \gamma_2\). Thus, we need to prove that \( \gamma_1 * \gamma_2 \simeq \gamma_2 * \gamma_1\).

\begin{align} \gamma_1 * \gamma_2 =& \begin{cases} \gamma_1(2s) & \text{if } s \in [0,1/2] \\ \gamma_2(2s-1) & \text{if } s \in [1/2,1] \end{cases} \\ \end{align}

\begin{align} \gamma_2 * \gamma_1 =& \begin{cases} \gamma_2(2s) & \text{if } s \in [0,1/2] \\ \gamma_1(2s-1) & \text{if } s \in [1/2,1] \end{cases} \\ \end{align}

A homotopy \( H (t,s)\) between them can be given as -

\begin{align} H(t,s) = \begin{cases} \gamma_1(2s(1-t)) \cdot \gamma_2 (2st) & \text{if } s \in [0,1/2] \\ \gamma_2((2s-1)(1-t)) \cdot \gamma_1((2s-1)t) & \text{if } s \in [1/2,1]. \end{cases} \end{align}

This satisfies the homotpy conditions, and hence \( \gamma_1 * \gamma_2 \simeq \gamma_2 * \gamma_1\). Therefore, \( \pi_1(G,x_0)\) is abelian.
