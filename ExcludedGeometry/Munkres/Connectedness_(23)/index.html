Problem 1

Let \(\tau\) and \(\tau'\) be two topologies on X. If \(\tau \supset \tau' \), what does connectedness of X in one topology imply about connectedness in the other ?

Given that \(\tau \supset \tau'\), if $X$ is connected in \(\tau'\Rightarrow \nexists \)U and V open in \(\tau' \Rightarrow \nexists \)U and V open in \(\tau\). Thus, if X is connected in \(\tau'\), then X is connected in \(\tau\).

Problem 2

We need to prove that \( \cup A_n\) is connected.

Let \( Y = \cup A_n \), and suppose \( Y = C \cup D\) such that \( C, D\) are non-empty, open, and \( C \cap D = \phi \). Since \( A_1 \) is connected, let \( A_1 \subset C\) as \(A_1 \) is connected. However, \( A_1 \cap A_2 = \phi\) \( \Rightarrow\) \( A_2 \subset C\), similarly \( A_3 \subset C\), ad infinitum. Therefore \( Y \cup D = \phi\), which is a contradiction. Hence, \( Y \) has no separation, and thus it is connected.

Problem 3

Let \(Y = A \cup (\cup A_{\alpha} = C \cup D \), where C and D are disjoint, non-empty, and open in Y. Now, \( A \subset C\), as \( A\) is connected, also \(A_{\alpha} \subset C\), as \( A \cap A_\alpha \neq \phi\) for all \( \alpha \) and \( A_\alpha\) is connected. Therefore \( Y \cap D = \phi \), which is a contradiction, and hence Y is connected.

Problem 4

\( \tau_f\) is a finite complement topology of X if it contains all subsets \(U\) of X such that \( X -U\) is either finite or all of X. It is given that X is infinite. Now, suppose X is disconnected, therefore \( X = A \cup B\) such that A and B are disjoint, non-empty, and open (as well as closed) in \( \tau_f\). If A is closed, then \((X-A) = B\) should be open, which implies B is finite. Similarly, A can be argued to be finite. But X is infinite, and hence could not be equal to union of two finite sets. Therefore, X has to be connected in finite complement topology.

Problem 6

\(A,C \subset X\), \( C\cap A \neq \phi, and \quad C \cap (X-A) \neq \phi \) . We need to prove that if C is a connected space, then C intersects Bd A.

Suppose C is connected, and does not intersect Bd A \( \Rightarrow C \cap \overline{A} = \phi, \quad C \cap \overline{(X-A)} = \phi\). Therefore, one can write \( C = (C \cap int(A)) \cup (C \cap int(X-A) )\), where both sets are non-empty, disjoint, and open in C (as int (A) and int (X-A) is open in X) which contradicts that C is connected. Thus, C intersects Bd A.

Problem 11

\( p : X \mapsto Y\) is a quotient map. Suppose that each set \( p^{-1} \{y\}\) is connected, and Y is connected, then X should be connected. Let \( U = p^{-1}\{y\}\), thus each such U is connected. Suppose X is disconnected, therefore \( X = A \cup B\), where both A and B are non-empty disjoint open sets in topology of A. As U is connected, all such \( p^{-1} \{y\}\) should be contained in either A or B. Thus, A and B are what we call (saturated sets) , and a quotient map maps saturated sets of X to open sets of Y. Thus, \( Y = p(A) \cup p(B)\), where \( p(A), p(B) \) are open in Y. This is a contradiction as Y is connected, hence X should be connected.
